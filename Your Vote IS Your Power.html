Register to Vote

Registering takes less than 10 minutes. Here's everything you need to get started, no matter what state you're in.

It's Easier Than You Think

Most states allow online registration. Here's the simple process:

  1. Check if you're already registered at Vote.org
  2. Gather your ID — driver's license or last 4 of SSN
  3. Fill out the online form for your state
  4. Submit and save your confirmation
  5. Mark your calendar for Election Day

Know Your Voting Rights

Federal law protects your right to vote. Understanding these protections is the first step to making sure your voice is heard.

⚖️

Right to Vote Without Intimidation

Federal law prohibits voter intimidation. No one — including election officials, poll watchers, or other voters — may threaten, coerce, or pressure you at the polls. If you feel intimidated, you can report it to the Department of Justice at 1-800-253-3931.

🗣️

Right to Language Assistance

Under the Voting Rights Act, jurisdictions with significant language minority populations must provide ballots and voting materials in those languages. You also have the right to bring someone to help you vote — except your employer or union representative.

♿

Right to Accessible Voting

The Americans with Disabilities Act requires polling places to be physically accessible. If you have a disability, you're entitled to assistance and accommodations, including accessible voting machines and curbside voting where available.

📊

Right to a Provisional Ballot

If your name doesn't appear on the voter rolls or your eligibility is questioned, you have the right to cast a provisional ballot under the Help America Vote Act. Your vote will be counted once your eligibility is confirmed.

⏰

Right to Time Off to Vote

Many states require employers to give workers time off to vote. Policies vary — some provide paid leave, others unpaid. Check your state's laws. Your employer cannot penalize you for taking legally allowed time to vote.

🛡️

Right to Report Problems

If you encounter voting issues — broken machines, long waits, or discriminatory practices — you can report them. Call the Election Protection Hotline at 866-OUR-VOTE (866-687-8683) for nonpartisan help in English, or 888-VE-Y-VOTA for Spanish.

Key Issues That Matter

Understanding the policy areas that directly impact Black communities is essential to making informed choices at the ballot box.

Healthcare

Healthcare Access & Equity

From Medicaid expansion to maternal health disparities, healthcare policy directly shapes health outcomes. Black Americans experience disproportionate rates of chronic conditions, and policy decisions on insurance coverage, hospital funding, and community health centers have life-or-death implications.

Learn more at KFF →
Economy

Economic Opportunity & Wealth

The racial wealth gap remains one of the most significant economic challenges. Policies around minimum wage, housing discrimination, small business access, and student debt all play critical roles in determining economic mobility and generational wealth building.

Research at Brookings →
Education

Education Funding & Access

School funding formulas, higher education access, and early childhood programs all shape future opportunities. Policies around Title I funding, HBCUs, Pell Grants, and K-12 resource equity have outsized impact on Black students and communities.

Explore at Education Trust →
Justice

Criminal Justice Reform

From policing practices and sentencing reform to reentry support and eliminating cash bail — criminal justice policy disproportionately affects Black communities. Understanding where candidates stand on reform is essential to informed voting.

Data at Sentencing Project →
Housing

Fair Housing & Homeownership

Housing policy — from zoning laws to mortgage lending — has historically been used to enforce segregation. Today, addressing redlining's legacy, expanding affordable housing, and protecting renters' rights remain critical policy priorities.

Learn at NFHA →
Voting Rights

Voting Rights & Access

Voter ID laws, gerrymandering, polling place closures, and voter purges continue to affect access to the ballot. Staying informed about legislative efforts to expand or restrict voting rights is fundamental to civic engagement.

Our Legacy

Milestones in Voting Rights

The fight for voting rights has been long, hard-won, and ongoing. Here are some of the defining moments.

1870
The 15th Amendment is ratified, prohibiting the denial of the right to vote based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude — though enforcement remained deeply flawed for nearly a century.
1920
The 19th Amendment grants women the right to vote, though Black women in many Southern states continued to face barriers such as poll taxes, literacy tests, and violence at the polls.
1964
The 24th Amendment abolishes poll taxes in federal elections — a major tool used to disenfranchise Black voters. The Civil Rights Act is also signed, prohibiting discrimination based on race in public accommodations and federally funded programs.
1965
The Voting Rights Act is signed into law by President Lyndon B. Johnson following the Selma to Montgomery marches. It outlawed literacy tests and provided federal oversight of voter registration in areas with historic discrimination.
1971
The 26th Amendment lowers the voting age to 18, expanding the electorate significantly and empowering young Americans of all backgrounds to participate in democracy.
1993
The National Voter Registration Act ("Motor Voter Act") makes voter registration easier by allowing people to register at DMVs and public agencies, expanding access for millions.
2013
In Shelby County v. Holder, the Supreme Court strikes down a key provision of the Voting Rights Act, removing federal preclearance requirements. Many states subsequently passed restrictive voting laws.
2020
Record voter turnout — over 159 million Americans cast ballots. Black voter mobilization played a decisive role in several key states. The election also sparked renewed national conversation about voting access and election security.

Organizations Making a Difference

These non-partisan and community-focused organizations are doing critical work in voter engagement, civil rights, and civic empowerment.

NAACP — Power the Vote
One of the nation's oldest civil rights organizations, the NAACP runs voter registration drives, advocates for voting rights legislation, and monitors elections across the country.
Visit NAACP →
Black Voters Matter Fund
Works to increase civic power in marginalized, predominantly Black communities through voter registration, advocacy, and grassroots organizing across 25+ states.
Visit BVM →
NAACP Legal Defense Fund
Fights for voting rights through litigation, advocacy, and public education. Their Democracy Defended reports document barriers to voting and provide resources for communities.
Visit LDF →
Black Girls Vote
A non-partisan organization dedicated to representing the concerns of Black women through voter education, engagement, and empowerment initiatives across the country.
Visit BGV →
Black Male Voter Project
Addresses historically low civic engagement among Black men through unconventional outreach — focus groups, community events, and culturally resonant organizing strategies.
Visit BMVP →
Vote.org
The largest non-partisan voter registration and GOTV technology platform in America. Provides tools for registration, absentee ballots, polling place lookups, and election reminders.
Visit Vote.org →
Brennan Center for Justice
A leading policy institute focused on voting rights, election security, and democratic reform. Publishes in-depth research and advocates for laws that expand ballot access.
Visit Brennan Center →
National Urban League
Advocates for economic and civic empowerment for African Americans through education, employment, health, and civic engagement programs in over 90 communities nationwide.
Visit NUL →
When We All Vote
Co-chaired by Michelle Obama, this non-partisan initiative works to increase voter participation in every election through community organizing, volunteer engagement, and voter education tools.
Visit WWAV →
